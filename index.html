Types of Geological Hazards

1. Landslide

A landslide involves rock, dirt, or other earth materials sliding down a slope, triggered by storms, earthquakes, volcanoes, or various factors.

Three major causes of landslides:

•       Geology

•       Morphology

•       Human Activity

Most Common Landslide in the Philippines: Rainfall-induced Landslides

Rainfall-induced landslides occur when prolonged or heavy rainfall saturates an area, increasing the weight of the landmass with the added water.

2. Sinkholes

Sinkholes occur when the ground's surface layer collapses, often due to water dissolving the underlying rocks. Natural sinkholes result from water erosion, while human activities like mining, drilling, construction, or broken water pipes can cause man-made sinkholes.

Three types of Sinkholes:

•       Solution Sinkholes: These form in areas with a thin soil cover, exposing bedrock to water erosion. Over time, water collects in spaces, leading to bedrock collapse.

•       Cover Subsidence Sinkholes: Occur when materials like clay or sand above the bedrock are not compact, leading to erosion and holes as these materials fill in the gaps.

•       Cover Collapse Sinkholes: These happen when the bedrock cannot support the weight of the soil above it, leading to sudden ground collapse as the bedrock weakens.

Warning Signs of Sinkholes:

•       New cracks in building foundations and homes

•       Cracks appearing in the ground

•       Depressions forming at ground level

•       Sudden emergence of a crater

Geological Maps and Hazard Mitigation

Geological hazard maps play a vital role in identifying regions at risk of earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and landslides. These tools enable scientists and local officials to predict possible hazards and inform the public about potential risks. One key feature of these maps is the legend, which details the severity of disasters in specific areas. For instance, regarding rainfall-induced landslides, the legend helps to gauge the susceptibility of various regions, allowing for informed preparation and mitigation strategies. This targeted approach fosters enhanced resilience within communities against geological threats.

Preparation and Response to Landslides

Before a Landslide:

1.      Develop an emergency plan.

2.      Determine if your location is landslide-prone.

3.      Stay informed about weather forecasts.

4.      Discuss emergency strategies with family and neighbors.

5.      Understand your area's landslide risks.

6.      Prepare emergency contacts and kits.

During a Landslide:

1.      Listen to alerts on battery-powered radios.

2.      Look out for unusual sounds and moving debris.

3.      Quickly move away from potential danger zones.

4.      Exercise caution near water bodies and during heavy rains.

After a Landslide:

1.                   Seek refuge in evacuation centers if necessary.

2.                   Steer clear of the affected area.

3.                   Stay updated with local news.

4.                   Report any damages to the authorities.

5.                   Inspect properties for structural damage.

6.                   Consult professionals for risk reduction advice. Preparation and Response to Sinkholes

Before a Sinkhole:

1.      Look for property cracks.

2.      Check for suddenly visible underground items.

3.      Observe for circular ground cracks.

4.      Notice the formation of new ponds.

5.      Be alert to trees falling or sudden ground shifts.

6.      Monitor for disrupted utility services.

During a Sinkhole:

1.      Avoid the vicinity of surface cracks.

2.      Report large holes to authorities immediately.

3.      Ensure children's safety.

4.      Cover and mark small holes.

5.      Document the site from a safe distance.

6.      Prevent non-experts from examining the site.

After a Sinkhole:

1.      Refrain from filling it with waste.

2.      Avoid building close to it.

3.      Seek professional help.

Hydrometeorological Hazards

Hydrometeorological hazards, resulting from weather, water, and atmospheric conditions, pose significant threats including loss of life, property damage, social upheaval, and environmental degradation. The Philippines, given its geography, is especially prone to these hazards, often facing multiple types simultaneously.

Signs of Hydrometeorological Hazards

A typhoon, a powerful tropical cyclone in the Western Pacific, becomes a super typhoon with winds surpassing 220 km/h. Signs of an approaching typhoon include:

1.      Ocean swell increasing, with waves becoming faster and taller.

2.      A transition from cumulus to thick, dark clouds, indicating imminent rain.

3.      A steady decrease in barometric pressure 36 hours before the storm.

4.      Sudden wind speed increases, amplifying ocean swell.

Thunderstorms

Thunderstorms, defined by lightning and thunder, can bring severe winds and heavy rain, and sometimes hail or tornadoes. Early signs include:

1.      Formation of cumulonimbus clouds from uneven heating.

2.      Darkening skies with clouds turning gray or green.

3.      The occurrence of lightning and thunder, hinting at an approaching storm.

4.      Wind gusts signaling the storm's imminent arrival.

Storm Surges and Floods

Storm surges, sudden rises in sea level caused by typhoons, share typhoon warning signs but are distinct from tsunamis. Residents in coastal areas should evacuate promptly.

Floods and flash floods, often triggered by heavy rainfall, cover dry land with water. Signs include weather predictions of heavy rain and sudden water rise, indicating potential flooding or flash flooding.

Hydrometeorological Hazard Maps

These maps pinpoint areas at risk from storm surges, typhoons, and floods, aiding in damage prevention and safeguarding lives by highlighting at-risk areas.

Tools for Monitoring Hydrometeorological Hazards by the Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical & Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA)

PAGASA, the national authority for meteorological and hydrological services in the Philippines, plays a critical role in ensuring public safety through timely warnings and monitoring of environmental conditions. They employ a variety of instruments for this purpose, including thermometers, anemometers, barometers, rain gauges, and sling psychrometers.

Instrument Descriptions:

•       Barograph: Continuously records atmospheric pressure.

•       Thermograph: Charts air temperature over time.

•       Ceiling Light Projector: Illuminates clouds to measure their height.

•       Clinometer: Assesses cloud base height from the ground.

•       Ceiling Balloon: Uses a hydrogen-filled balloon to determine cloud height.

•       Theodolite: Tracks wind speed and direction with a pilot balloon.

•       Radiosonde: Sends back temperature, pressure, and humidity data.

•       Rawinsonde: Adds wind velocity measurements to atmospheric data.  Wind Finding Radar: Analyzes wind speed and direction via radar.

•       Weather Surveillance Radar: Tracks typhoons and clouds up to 400 kilometers away.

•       Doppler Radar: Identifies precipitation movement and storm structures.

•       Weather Satellite: Offers a comprehensive view of weather patterns, collecting data from distant stations.

Fire Hazards: Understanding and Prevention

Fire, a rapid oxidation process, emits light and heat and has been pivotal in human progress. While beneficial for heat and power, fire in nature can also aid ecological balance by clearing pests and undergrowth. Fire involves heat, fuel, and oxygen, igniting when the fuel reaches its ignition point. Removing one of these elements can extinguish the fire. Fire stages include the incipient, growth, fully developed, and decay stages, each presenting unique challenges and responses.

Common Fire Causes:

1.      Faulty electrical wiring

2.      LPG issues

3.      Unattended cooking

4.      Careless smoking

5.      Neglected open flames

6.      Improperly stored flammables

7.      Spontaneous combustion

8.      Mismanaged pyrotechnics

9.      Lightning

Fire Safety Measures:

•       Never leave flames unattended.

•       Inspect electrical equipment routinely.

•       Keep flammables away from heat sources.

•       Educate on fire safety.

•       Have fire extinguishers and sand buckets ready.

•       Install smoke detectors.

•       Practice evacuation drills and have a fire plan.

Emergency Response:

•       Leave smoke-filled areas immediately.

•       Alert the fire department promptly.

•       Use the STOP, DROP, & ROLL technique if your clothes catch fire. R.A.C.E. and Fire Extinguisher Use:

•       R.A.C.E.: Rescue, Alarm, Contain, Extinguish.  P.A.S.S.: Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep.

Creating Effective Fire Safety and Evacuation Plans:

Ensure clear escape routes, mark exits visibly, provide adequate exits, install emergency doors and lighting, train everyone on evacuation procedures, and designate a safe meeting point.

Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR) and Management (DRRM):

•       Disaster: Significant disruptions that exceed a community's ability to cope.

•       Risk: The likelihood and potential impact of adverse events.

•       Hazard: Natural or human-induced elements that can cause damage.

•       Vulnerability: Factors making a community susceptible to harm.

•       Capacity: Resources and abilities to mitigate disaster risks.

•       Exposure: The extent to which elements are likely to face hazard events.

•       Disaster Risk Reduction: Strategies to minimize disaster risks through careful management of hazards, vulnerabilities, and capacities.

•       Disaster Risk Reduction and Management: Coordinated efforts to reduce disaster impacts and enhance resilience, emphasizing shared responsibility and integration into broader planning and development strategies.

Pre-Event Strategies

1.   Prevention: Taking proactive steps to completely avoid the negative impact of hazards through smart land use and engineering. While total prevention might not always be possible, shifting towards mitigation is the next best step.

1.   Mitigation: Aiming to reduce the severity of the negative effects that disasters have. This incorporates a range of strategies, from engineering solutions to public awareness campaigns, to lessen the impacts of hazards.

1.   Adaptation: Adjusting both natural and human systems in anticipation of or in response to changing climate conditions to minimize damage and seize any beneficial opportunities.

1.   Preparedness: Developing the knowledge and capabilities among governments, organizations, communities, and individuals to effectively anticipate, respond to, and recover from the impacts of potential or actual disasters.

Post-Event Actions

1.   Response: Providing immediate assistance after a disaster to save lives, reduce health hazards, ensure public safety, and meet the basic needs of those affected. This phase focuses on short-term, immediate relief.

1.   Recovery: Working towards the restoration and improvement of the living conditions in disaster-struck communities, emphasizing risk reduction in the rebuilding process.

Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Areas

Area 1: Prevention and Mitigation

 Actions include early warning systems, hazard mapping, and interventions.  Led by the Department of Science and Technology (DOST).

Area 2: Preparedness

•       Focuses on building knowledge and capacity for disaster response.

•       Managed by the Department of Interior and Local Government (DILG).

Area 3: Response

•       Supportive services for affected communities, aiming to save lives and fulfill basic needs.

•       Coordinated by the Department of Social Welfare and Development (DSWD).

Area 4: Rehabilitation and Recovery

•       Efforts to rebuild and restore, aiming to reduce future disaster risks.

•       Overseen by the National Economic and Development Authority (NEDA).

Important Activities:

•       Early warning systems, hazard mappings, and community-based initiatives play crucial roles across all areas.

Importance of Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR):

1.      Monitoring and assessing disaster risks to enhance early warning.

2.      Reducing risk factors.

3.      Building a culture of safety and resilience.

4.      Ensuring preparedness at all levels for effective response and recovery.

Community-Based DRR Management

•       Allocation of resources, development of tools, and integration of policies for disaster risk reduction and climate change adaptation are essential. Training, capacity building, and public information dissemination are key strategies.

Family and Community Preparedness Plans

•       Identify potential hazards and plan accordingly.

•       Strengthen your home's structure and have a communication plan in place.

•       Maintain a 72-hour survival kit with essentials.

Barangay Disaster Preparedness Steps:

•       Gather critical barangay information and hazard assessments.

•       Outline specific pre-, during, and post-disaster actions.  Identify safe evacuation areas.

The 72-Hour Survival Kit and DRRM Law

•       The kit should include first aid, food, water, tools, and important documents.

•       Republic Act No. 10121 strengthens disaster management in the Philippines, setting up a comprehensive framework and emphasizing local and national coordination.

By understanding and implementing these strategies and legal frameworks, communities can enhance their resilience against disasters, effectively manage risks, and ensure a coordinated response and recovery effort.
